The fundamental theorem of calculus (FTC) establishes a profound connection between differentiation and integration. It states that if a function \( f \) is continuous on an interval \([a, b]\), then:

1. FTC Part 1:
The function \( F \) defined by the integral of \( f \) from \( a \) to \( x \), \[ F(x) = \int_{a}^{x} f(t) \, dt, \] is differentiable on \((a, b)\), and its derivative is the original function \( f \): \[ F'(x) = f(x). \]
2. FTC Part 2:
If \( F \) is any antiderivative of \( f \) on \([a, b]\) (meaning \( F' = f \)), then the definite integral of \( f \) from \( a \) to \( b \) is given by: \[ \int_{a}^{b} f(t) \, dt = F(b) - F(a). \] In probability theory, particularly in the study of continuous random variables, we define:

- Probability Density Function (pdf): A function \( f(x) \) that describes the relative likelihood of a random variable \( X \) taking on a particular value \( x \). It satisfies \( f(x) \geq 0 \) and \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \, dx = 1\).

- Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF): A function \( F(x) \) that gives the probability that \( X \) is less than or equal to \( x \): \[ F(x) = \mathbb{P}(X \leq x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(t) \, dt. \] Connecting FTC to Density Functions and CDFs:

The relationship between the pdf and the CDF mirrors the relationship described in the FTC:

1. Integration of pdf to get CDF: By integrating the pdf \( f(t) \) from \( -\infty \) to \( x \), we obtain the CDF \( F(x) \): \[ F(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(t) \, dt. \] This is analogous to defining \( F(x) \) as the integral of \( f \), as in the FTC Part 1.

2. Differentiation of CDF to get pdf: If the pdf \( f(x) \) is continuous, then by the FTC, the derivative of the CDF is the pdf: \[ F'(x) = \frac{d}{dx} F(x) = f(x). \] This demonstrates that the rate of change of the cumulative probability up to \( x \) is given by the density at \( x \).
