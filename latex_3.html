To formally illustrate why the median minimizes the sum of absolute deviations \( \sum_{i=1}^{N} |x_i - c| \) with respect to a generic value \( c \), we proceed as follows: $$ S(c) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} |x_i - c| $$ We want to find \( c \) such that \( S(c) \) is minimized. Let \( X \) be a random variable representing the values of the observations \( x_i \). We can analyze the expected value \( \mathbb{E}[|X - c|] \). We take the derivative with respect to \( c \): $$ \frac{d}{dc}\mathbb{E}[|X - c|] = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{d}{dc}|X - c|\right] = \mathbb{E}[-\operatorname{sgn}(X - c)], $$ where \( \operatorname{sgn}(X - c) \) is the sign function, taking values: $$ -1 \Leftrightarrow ( X < c ), $$ $$ 0 \Leftrightarrow ( X = c ), $$ $$ 1 \Leftrightarrow ( X > c ). $$ We can express the expected value in terms of probabilities: $$ \frac{d}{dc}\mathbb{E}[|X - c|] = P(X < c) - P(X > c), $$ where \( P(X < c) \) is the probability that \( X \) is less than \( c \) and \( P(X > c) \) is the probability that \( X \) is greater than \( c \). By the normalization properties of probability, we know: $$ P(X > c) = 1 - P(X < c). $$ Substituting this, we get: $$ \frac{d}{dc}\mathbb{E}[|X - c|] = P(X < c) - (1 - P(X < c)) = 2P(X < c) - 1. $$ For the derivative to be zero (which corresponds to a minimum or maximum point), it must hold that: $$ 2P(X < c) - 1 = 0 \Rightarrow P(X < c) = \frac{1}{2}. $$ This implies that \( c \) must be such that half of the observations are below \( c \) and half are above, meaning \( c \) is the median of the dataset. We have thus proven that the median \( Me \) minimizes the sum of absolute deviations: $$ \sum_{i=1}^{N} |x_i - Me| \leq \sum_{i=1}^{N} |x_i - c| \quad \forall c. $$ This property arises because, at the point \( c \) equal to the median, the deviations from the values above and below \( c \) balance out, making the sum of absolute deviations minimal.
