Observations and Results

After performing the simulations with varying sample sizes \( n \) and number of samples \( m \), we observe the following:

Mean of Sampling Averages (\( \overline{X}_{\text{mean}} \)):

The mean of the sample means (\( \overline{X}_{\text{mean}} \)) closely approximates the theoretical mean (\( \mu \)) of the parent distribution.
As the number of samples \( m \) increases, \( \overline{X}_{\text{mean}} \) converges to \( \mu \).

Variance of Sampling Averages (\( \sigma_{\overline{X}}^2 \)):

The variance of the sample means is approximately equal to the theoretical variance divided by the sample size (\( \sigma_{\overline{X}}^2 \approx \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \)).
Increasing the sample size \( n \) leads to a decrease in \( \sigma_{\overline{X}}^2 \), demonstrating that sample means become less variable with larger \( n \).

5. Relationship with the Parent Distribution

Convergence of Means:

The consistency between \( \overline{X}_{\text{mean}} \) and \( \mu \) illustrates the Law of Large Numbers, which states that the sample mean converges in probability towards the expected value as the sample size increases.
With larger \( m \), the distribution of the sampling averages becomes more concentrated around \( \mu \), reflecting increased accuracy.

Convergence of Variances:

The relationship \( \sigma_{\overline{X}}^2 = \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \) indicates that the variability of the sampling averages decreases with larger sample sizes.
This phenomenon is a key aspect of the Central Limit Theorem, which also implies that the distribution of the sampling averages approaches a normal distribution as \( n \) increases, regardless of the parent distribution's shape.

6. Impact of Increasing Sample Size and Number of Samples

Effect of Increasing \( n \) (Sample Size):

Reduction in Variance: As \( n \) increases, \( \sigma_{\overline{X}}^2 \) decreases, leading to a tighter clustering of sample means around \( \mu \).
Approach to Normality: Larger \( n \) enhances the normality of the sampling distribution of the mean due to the Central Limit Theorem.

Effect of Increasing \( m \) (Number of Samples):

Stability of Estimates: A larger \( m \) provides a more reliable estimate of \( \overline{X}_{\text{mean}} \) and \( \sigma_{\overline{X}}^2 \).
Convergence to Theoretical Values: With more samples, the empirical distribution of sampling averages better approximates the theoretical predictions.

Combined Effect:

Increasing both \( n \) and \( m \) results in the distribution of sampling averages closely mirroring the theoretical normal distribution centered at \( \mu \) with variance \( \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \).
The mean of the sample means and the mean of the variances converge to the parent distribution's mean and the scaled variance, respectively.
